Chapter 1 — An Ordinary Tuesday
The house near the Savannah River woke up slowly, the way old houses do, carrying the habit of breathing before their people. The light of dawn came through the crack in the curtains in a thin strip and settled on her shoulder, and Jago Quint stood there looking at that shoulder for longer than was reasonable. Rowena slept on her side, one hand open near her face, like someone who had left a question half-asked and decided to finish asking it the next day. The sheet had slipped down to her waist, and the light caught the loose strands of her hair, which still smelled of the soap from the night before.
He got up without a sound. His body knew this ritual—getting out of bed without waking the one who stayed. There was a calm inside him that still scared him, six months after she'd moved into that house. That was love, he'd discovered, and at the same time it was something else: a small dread, almost decorative, that lived beneath his rib cage and beat along with his heart. He thought he knew how to handle it. A man who survived what he'd survived learns to tame his own fear.
He went down the stairs, stepping on the side of the wood that didn't creak. The kitchen was still cold, with that stale air of June mornings that hadn't decided whether they were warm or not. He turned on the kettle, measured out her coffee like someone writing a sentence he already knew by heart—two scoops of grounds, water that hadn't fully boiled, a little less than the usual ratio, because Rowena liked her coffee milder at the start of the day, as if the morning needed to ask permission before coming in.
The smell rose slowly through the kitchen. There was something in it he could never quite name—it wasn't the coffee itself, it was what the smell stood for: she was still there, and she'd come down. There was a thought he avoided at that hour, and the thought showed up anyway: if she ever vanished from there, the smell of coffee would tear him apart with devastating efficiency. He stirred the spoon slowly and brushed the thought aside with the same gesture you'd use to wave off a polite fly. Not today.
The mail came early. Phelan, the personal assistant, delivered the envelope at the side door with the discreet punctuality Jago paid dearly for. There were three letters from the office, one from the management of one of the properties, and a cream-colored envelope, no return address, addressed to Rowena Pike in the impersonal handwriting of a secretary at some respectable institution. He recognized the letterhead in the upper corner before he even looked properly. He stood there for a second with the envelope between his fingers, feeling the specific weight of quality paper.
"I'll take this one," he said, low. He tucked the envelope into the inside pocket of his jacket as if he were putting away an important document he'd remember to hand over later. He knew he wouldn't remember. He'd already decided not to remember before he even finished the gesture. The worst part was that his hand had made the move without consulting him, with an old efficiency he recognized as his own and didn't like recognizing.
Phelan saw it. Phelan said nothing. The two exchanged a brief greeting, and the assistant left the same way he'd come in, his steps muffled by the runner in the hallway.
When Rowena came down, coffee was served in two cups. She appeared in the kitchen doorway in a long nightgown, her hair loosely pinned up, rubbing her eye with a closed fist like a child. The morning sun was coming in now through the side window and drew a golden square on the wooden floor between them. He looked up and the small decorative dread settled back beneath his rib cage, quiet.
"You got up early," she remarked, her voice still thick with sleep.
"I couldn't sleep with you breathing so beautifully like that," Jago answered, and his mouth almost smiled.
She laughed. It was a short, slightly husky laugh, the kind he collected inside without admitting he collected them. She sat on the stool at the kitchen island, took the cup with both hands, and smelled it before drinking, the way she did every day—her eyes closing for half a second over the steam, a gesture so hers that he already knew it by heart. He watched without seeming to watch.
"Are you expecting anything today?" he asked, and the tone was casual, in that practiced way.
Rowena thought for a second, her eyes still on the cup.
"An answer," she said. "Maybe. That conversation I told you about last week." She looked up. "You remember?"
"I remember," he answered.
He remembered. In his pocket, he had exactly the answer she was waiting for, and the answer was generous, and the answer would take her out of that house for six months. The pocket weighed on him in a specific way. He drank his own coffee and said nothing more.
Rowena looked at him for a second longer than her usual. Then she looked at her own coffee, and the rest of the morning ran along beautifully, the way their mornings usually ran.
At noon, Silas Ashford was waiting for Jago at the office in downtown Savannah. The lawyer had a way of sitting in chairs that didn't belong to him as if they did: legs crossed, one finger resting against his temple, the expressionless face he wore when he brought bad news. The air conditioning in the room was set too high, and the artificial chill clashed with the heat coming in through the wide windows overlooking the square.
"Short lunch," Silas announced when Jago walked in.
"Short why?"
"Because what I've got to tell you doesn't go well with dessert."
Jago closed the door behind him. He sat down on the other side of the desk. The two had known each other long enough to skip the runaround.
"Someone's asking questions," Silas said, without raising his voice. "Again. In three different places. Discreet questions, the kind a trained person asks. And the questions come too close to what we agreed to never let anyone get close to."
Jago didn't move. Inside, the decorative dread grew an inch.
"Name?" he asked.
"Not yet. But they pay well, and they pay with money that leaves no trail. That interests me more than the name."
Jago looked at the desk. There was a line of silence the two of them respected, the kind built over years of loyalty. Silas waited. He always waited, with that stone patience he charged dearly for by the hour.
"Double the security at the house," Jago said, finally. "Quietly. Rowena can't know."
Silas kept looking at him a second longer than necessary.
"Jago."
"She can't know, Silas."
The lawyer ran his tongue along the inside corner of his cheek, a small gesture he made when he wanted to say something and chose not to. Then he nodded, in that short, dry way of his.
"And that envelope?" Silas asked. "Did the answer come?"
Jago didn't answer right away. He tapped his thumb lightly on the tabletop, once, twice.
"It came."
"Are you going to deliver it?"
"I am."
"When?"
Jago looked up. There was something in his eyes that Silas had known long before this conversation—the same thing that lived in that gaze ten years ago, and that Silas had hoped, for a while, had gotten better.
"When I figure out how."
Silas nodded, but his face didn't agree. The two finished lunch in decent silence, with the noise from the square coming in muffled through the windows. When Jago left the office, the cream envelope was still weighing in the inside pocket of his jacket, and he had, for the first time in a long time, a brief urge to tear it up.
Rowena spent the afternoon in the living room of the house near the river. She was rereading some old work notes, things she kept in a hardcover folder that sat on a shelf in plain sight, no hiding place at all. She'd learned to value that transparency the way people do who have already lost someone by hiding too much. The afternoon light came in slanted through the half-open blinds and striped the rug in golden bands. Outside, the river made its usual gentle sound.
The living room phone rang at a little past five. Neve had gone to the market with Phelan. Jago hadn't come back yet. Rowena answered.
"Quint residence," she said, the way she always answered, half joking with the formality.
On the other end, a full second of silence. Then a man's voice, hoarse with age and with something harder to name:
"Good afternoon. I need to speak with Mr. Quint."
"He's not in at the moment. Can I take a message?"
Another silence. She heard the man breathe as if he were weighing every word before letting it out. Rowena, a well-trained journalist, began paying attention with her whole body, without yet knowing why. There was something in that silence that wasn't indecision. It was calculation.
"It's..." the man coughed lightly. "It's about Kit. Tell him it's about Kit. He'll understand."
Rowena stopped breathing for a second. Not because of the name—the name still meant nothing to her. She stopped because of the way the man said it. Like someone handing over a password he knows will open a door that's been locked for a long time.
"Kit?" she repeated, slowly, and her voice came out more neutral than she was feeling. "May I ask who's calling?"
The man was already answering when the front door slammed. Quick steps in the hallway, quicker than Jago's step usually was at the end of the day. He walked into the room with his meeting posture still in his body, and stopped walking when he saw the phone in her hand.
She watched the color drain from his face in real time. It was something she'd never seen before in that man—no anger, no practiced surprise, just a physical, animal whiteness, rising from his chin to his forehead in seconds, as if someone had flipped a switch.
"Who is it?" he asked, his voice too low for the size of the question.
Rowena didn't answer. She held out the receiver.
Jago took it. Brought it to his ear. Listened for two seconds. Closed his eyes once—a single blink longer than normal, the kind of gesture that isn't reflex, it's decision—and then said just one sentence:
"Don't call this number."
He hung up. He kept his hand on the phone for a length of time she counted inside her own chest, beat by beat.
Rowena looked at him. She knew that man in her body, in his smell, in the way he ran his thumb under his eye when he was tired. She'd never seen him white like that. She'd never seen him need support just to stay standing.
"Jago."
He didn't answer right away.
"Who was it?" she asked, with the trained calm her profession had given her as a gift.
He looked up. And that was when she saw it—for the first time since the two of them had met, she saw him decide to lie. It wasn't a quick decision. She saw the moment before it, the instant when he could still choose the other path.
"An old employee," Jago said. "Drunk, probably. Got the wrong number."
She kept looking at him. He held her gaze with the composure of someone who'd spent his whole life holding gazes he couldn't afford to lose. He knew, inside, that she knew. Still, he wanted her to let it go.
"He said a name," Rowena said, and her voice came out very low, almost gentle, which was the way she used when the question wasn't gentle.
Jago swallowed.
"What name?"
"Kit."
He didn't blink. He carried his body to the sideboard next to the sofa, rested his hand on the wood as if he needed something solid, and spoke looking at the river through the window, his back to her:
"I don't know him."
Rowena stood where she was. On the other side of the room, near the big window, there was a chair—just like the one in the mansion's main hall, brought here when they moved in. She had, now, six months of looking at that chair without understanding why no one ever sat in it, why the seat was always turned slightly toward the river, as if waiting for someone who knew where they'd arrive from. She looked at the chair, looked at Jago's back, looked at the chair again, and the chair, for the first time, had a sound.
The name was short. Short and old. It had only three letters and had slipped down the phone line like a poorly tied secret, spoken by a hoarse voice.
Kit.
She didn't repeat it out loud. She kept it. She was very good at that.
That night, Rowena didn't sleep. She lay looking at the bedroom ceiling, listening to his breathing beside her—that breathing she knew better than her own, that she could tell when it was real sleep and when it was faked. That night, for the first time, she couldn't read it.
Outside, the river ran with its usual soft sound, indifferent to what was happening inside that beautiful house. Inside her chest, an empty chair had begun to creak.
Chapter 2 — Tenderness Isn't Truth
The sun came into the bedroom in the morning as if nothing had happened, and Jago moved in bed the way he always did—his hand seeking her waist beneath the sheet, his face pressing into the nape of her neck, the short kiss at the spot where her hair began. Rowena lay still, eyes closed, and received it all with her skin. His warmth was real. The weight of his arm was real. It was easier to stay quiet a second longer and let her body believe in what her mind hadn't yet resolved.
Then she turned over.
"Jago."
"Hm."
"The call yesterday."
He opened his eyes with a staged laziness. She saw the staging—it was quick, almost imperceptible, but she saw it. She'd learned to see the small things in him before she'd learned their names.
"I already told you, my love. It was nobody."
"You went white."
Jago smiled with half his mouth, that small smile he reserved for defusing difficult situations in boardrooms. He raised his hand and tucked a strand of her hair behind her ear with a calculated lightness—a gesture that had always worked before, one she herself had loved in those first months, when she still couldn't tell genuine tenderness from the tenderness that served as a curtain.
"I didn't," he said, his voice low and firm. "You were against the light."
She looked at him in silence. A pause of three, four seconds, enough for any guilty person to fill the emptiness with more words. He didn't fill it. He was too good for that. He leaned in, kissed the corner of her mouth with a tenderness that was real and at the same time evasive about everything, and spoke against her skin:
"Let's have coffee."
Rowena let it go. She kissed him back, short. Got up. Went to shower alone, and while the water fell too hot on her shoulders, she thought for the first time, in plain terms, a sentence that bothered her a great deal: tenderness and truth are not the same thing.
She stayed under the water longer than she needed to. The bathroom smelled of the citrus soap Jago had started buying for her when he noticed which one she liked. One more detail he memorized. One more gesture she no longer knew how to receive.
She came down dressed to go out. Linen pants, a light-colored blouse, a small bag she used when she went downtown. Jago was in the kitchen reading something on the tablet, with a cup of coffee in front of him that must have been half lukewarm by now. He looked at her with that gaze that crossed the room without needing distance.
"Where are you going?"
She found it amusing. Not the question—the tone. It was his old tone, the one she thought had been left behind since they'd gotten themselves organized, six months earlier, to live together there near the river.
"Downtown. Buy a couple things. Marlowe arrives today, remember?"
"I remember." He set down the tablet. "Taking the car?"
"I'm driving myself."
He nodded as if he were accepting it. He reached out, took her hand on the cold marble counter, squeezed it lightly, his fingers wrapping around hers with the ease of someone who already knew every knuckle.
"Be careful."
"Always."
She went out the side door. She walked along the stone hallway to the garage, took the keys off the hook. The air outside smelled of the river's dampness and of some neighboring garden that must have had jasmine. It was early enough that Savannah's heat hadn't yet weighed down. When she opened the electric gate, two men in suits were standing outside, next to a dark car she didn't recognize. They didn't seem like a threat. They seemed like scenery—the scenery of another kind of house, not hers.
One of them approached with a smile that was too polite.
"Mrs. Pike, good morning. We'll be going with you."
Rowena blinked.
"Going with me where?"
"Wherever you go. House orders. Security."
She looked at the man. Looked at the other one. Neither of them seemed embarrassed. Neither of them seemed to expect her to argue. That, more than anything else, was what bothered her—that they'd arrived ready for the normalcy of a situation she hadn't authorized.
"I didn't ask for security," she said, with a calm that wasn't easy to maintain.
"You don't need to ask, ma'am. It's already been arranged."
She stood for a second on the stone pavement. Inside the house, behind her, Jago knew. Jago was on the tablet with the lukewarm cup and he knew. He'd given the order the day before, at the downtown office, and he knew the exact second she'd meet those two men at the gate. At that very moment, sitting in the kitchen, he swallowed some coffee that was too cold to cover his own nerves, his hand pressing lightly on the tablet for no reason at all.
Rowena didn't make a scene. She simply said:
"All right."
She got into her car. Let their car follow behind. She drove to downtown Savannah with both hands on the wheel and the silence inside the car so vast that she turned on the radio, found it unbearable, turned it off. Her hands stayed on the leather of the wheel, and she looked out the window at the old magnolias lining the avenue, white and heavy from so much blooming, and thought that some beautiful things were also suffocating.
Marlowe Trent showed up on time at the old café in the historic district, with a bag too big for anything she could possibly need there and an expression Rowena recognized from far off: the expression of someone who'd flown three hours to give a dose of reality to someone she loved very much and who was in need of hearing it.
"Row."
"Marlowe."
They hugged the way they'd hugged for years—tight, without ceremony, Marlowe rubbing her hand twice on her back with that energy of someone who'd arrived to fix something. The café smelled of cinnamon and burnt coffee from an old machine, and the dark wooden tables had marks from years of cups. They sat near the window. Marlowe ordered black coffee. Rowena, a cappuccino. Across the street, the dark car parked with professional discretion.
"Those two yours?" Marlowe pointed with her chin, no filter at all.
"They're the house's."
"The house's."
"Yeah."
Marlowe raised an eyebrow. Marlowe had an eyebrow that asked whole questions on its own, without needing anything more.
"Row."
"Don't start."
"Row, love doesn't treat you like a hostage."
Rowena laughed. It was a short laugh, a little awkward, but honest—and she felt, along with it, a good pang, the pang of someone being reminded of herself by someone who'd known her before all of this.
They talked for an hour. Marlowe talked about the newsroom, about the new editor who'd reorganized everything, about the people who still asked about Rowena now and then with that mix of curiosity and respect that comes just before being forgotten. Rowena listened and wanted to feel a clean kind of longing, uncomplicated, but the longing that arrived had an uneven edge. At some point, out of old reflex, she took the recorder from her bag. A small, silver recorder that she carried out of a habit of more than six years, always in the side pocket. She looked at the device in the palm of her hand as if she weren't sure when it had ended up there. She put it back without pressing a single button.
Marlowe saw it. Marlowe saw everything, always had.
"Who are you now, journalist?" she asked, with the corner of her mouth crooked in a way that was both a question and affection.
Rowena laughed for real this time. A bigger laugh, that arrived without warning.
"A woman waiting for an answer that never comes."
"What answer?"
"A proposal. A job offer. They sent it last week. I still haven't gotten any reply."
Marlowe kept looking at her. She held the cup with both hands and waited a second longer before speaking—that second Rowena knew, the second that came before the sentences that stayed.
"Row."
"What."
"Are you sure it didn't come?"
Rowena stopped the cappuccino midway. The cup hung suspended, the heat rising to her face.
"What do you mean?"
"Nothing," Marlowe deflected, and looked at the dark car across the street with an expression that was nothing. "Never mind."
But the sentence stayed. Marlowe knew how to put sentences in the air the way they'd hang suspended, and this one stayed the rest of the coffee, the rest of the way back, the rest of the whole afternoon.
Rowena returned to the house near the river in the late afternoon, with Savannah's golden light striking the paving stones from the side and a thin mist rising from the river as it always did when the heat began to give way. The two men followed behind in professional silence. Jago was still at the downtown office. The house was quiet—only Neve in the kitchen, cutting something with that silent precision of hers, and the smell of rosemary coming from some pot that had been on the stove for a while.
Rowena went into Jago's private study with an excuse she'd prepared for herself while still in the car: she needed that old contract from the house renovation work, the carpenter had asked for a copy of the original specification. It was a polite lie, the kind you tell yourself before doing what you were going to do anyway.
She opened the first drawer. Office papers, all in order, labeled with the precision of a man who tolerated chaos on no surface. Second drawer. Contracts, thin folders with dates and numbers. Third drawer.
A cream-colored envelope, no return address, with her name in the center in the impersonal, typewritten handwriting. Letterhead in the upper right corner. Winslow Foundation for Independent Journalism.
The envelope was open.
She stood still. The study itself went still around her—the dark shelves, the books lined up by size, the narrow window that looked out onto the side garden with the moss on the stones. Her hands went strangely quiet. She took the envelope. She pulled out the contents carefully, as if the paper might confirm something she already knew but didn't yet want to name.
Two sheets. The first was the formal invitation—a job offer, a series of long investigative reports, full grant, complete autonomy over the topics. An offer that would bring her career back to life from scratch, at the exact point where she'd left off before Savannah, before the ball, before everything.
The second sheet was a carbon copy. A reply. Dated ten days ago.
Dear Sirs, we thank you for your generous consideration. At this time, Miss Pike is not available to take on new professional commitments. We renew our thanks.
A signature at the bottom in handwriting. The same handwriting that appeared in the notes he left her in the kitchen in the morning, the same as the contracts she signed alongside him, the same one she'd learned to recognize before even reading what was written. His handwriting.
Rowena kept looking at the sheet for a length of time she couldn't measure. Her body didn't tremble. Her body went very still, and the stillness was heavier than any tremor.
Rowena put the envelope back in the drawer. Closed it with the same care she'd opened it. She left the private study without taking the carpenter's contract. She didn't need the contract. She never had.
She went to the living room. She sat on the sofa, without turning on the light. The afternoon had closed into that dark blue of early night that came fast in Savannah's summer, and the room filled with a warm dimness. On the other side, next to the big window, the empty chair was in its usual place, with the last light of day reaching only the backrest.
She looked at the chair for a long time. Two stories, she thought. One of them has a short, old name that no one pronounces inside this house. The other has his handwriting on a sheet that answered for her without her knowing.
The phone rang. It was Jago.
"Love. I'm not going to make it home yet. Don't stay hungry, eat without me."
She closed her eyes. She held her voice in the right place, that exact place where the voice stops trembling and becomes just an answer.
"Okay."
"I love you."
"You too."
She hung up. She kept the phone in her hand, the heat of the device against her palm. Outside, the river ran with that low, constant sound she'd learned to find calming and that now sounded different. The empty chair waited. And, for the first time in six months, Rowena Pike felt, inside her own chest, the low, firm creak of a decision beginning to be made.
Chapter 3 — The Name No One Said
The cream envelope sat on the table between them as if it weighed more than the wood holding it up. Rowena had waited all day to set it down there, at the exact spot where he usually left his keys. She chose the dining room table because it was the only room where Jago couldn't lean on a work object. No paper, no pen, no phone. Just him, her, and his handwriting betraying his handwriting.
Jago came in after eight. He took off his jacket with the slowness of someone who already knew what he'd find. The house near the Savannah River smelled of jasmine from the garden outside, an out-of-season sweetness that only worsened the sensation that the air was breathing on its own. He saw the envelope. He didn't ask what it was. Inside, he felt a lying sentence form before he could stop it—I was going to tell you—and in the same instant knew it was false. He wasn't going to. If she hadn't found it, that paper would have died at the bottom of that drawer.
"Rowena," his voice came out too controlled to be sincere. "It's not what it looks like."
She crossed her arms over her linen dress. She didn't sit. The yellow light of the chandelier fell between them like a border.
"It's exactly what it looks like, Jago."
He breathed in deep. The cold panic settled between one rib and another, that old cold he knew from another night, ten years ago. He swallowed twice before he managed to speak.
"I wanted to protect you."
"From a job offer."
"From an offer that was going to take you away from Savannah for six months."
Rowena smiled without joy.
"And the decision to go or stay was yours?"
He opened his mouth and found no answer. She continued, lower, because the low voice hurt more.
"You answered for me. You said thank you for me. You declined for me." Each sentence fell like a small stone into the bottom of a glass. "And then you brought me breakfast."
"Rowena…"
"No." She raised her hand, and her hand was trembling, and she hated that. "You're not going to ask me for time. Time is the one thing you shouldn't ask for today. You had six months."
He took a step toward her. She took a step back. It was a small movement, but he felt as if he'd been shoved against the opposite wall of the room. Inside, something in him—the part that was always certain—began to crack. He watched her mouth pronounce the next sentence before he heard it.
"Who is Kit?"
The name fell to the dining room floor and stayed there, gleaming. Jago looked at the envelope, looked at her, looked at the window that faced the dark river outside. He said nothing. And his silence was the first real confession.
"Good night, Jago."
She went up the stairs slowly. He stood beside the table for too long, listening to her footsteps disappear into the upstairs hallway. Then he sat in the nearest chair, elbows on his knees, and stared at his own envelope as if the paper were capable of answering for him.
Rowena didn't sleep. She spent the small hours with her eyes open at the ceiling of the guest room—that room she'd never entered before, and where the sheets smelled of forgotten lavender. She mentally repeated the list of things she knew. The name. The chair. The terrace. Eudora's remark about a laugh that wasn't hers. The payments she hadn't yet traced. The envelope answered in his handwriting.
There were two stories being hidden. She needed to unravel both.
In the morning, before the sun rose fully, she called a taxi from the street. She didn't ask for a house car. She didn't tell Phelan. She put on the plainest clothes she had and went out the side door, and no guard saw her—because no guard expected her to leave before six in the morning.
Silas Ashford's office was in downtown Savannah, in an old townhouse with tall windows and a permanent smell of coffee and old paper. The sun hadn't yet warmed the sidewalk when she climbed the steps. Silas answered the door himself. He didn't even ask what she wanted. He just pointed to the chair in front of the desk.
"You already know why I'm here."
"I'm pretty sure I do." Silas poured two coffees without asking. "And I'm pretty sure I shouldn't open my mouth."
Rowena accepted the coffee and didn't drink it.
"Silas." Her voice came out firmer than she expected. "You're his friend. I know that. But you were also the only one who looked me in the eye that night at the ball and didn't pretend not to see what you were seeing. I need to know who Kit is."
Silas let the coffee rest between his hands. He stared at the tabletop for long enough that Rowena understood the answer would hurt him too.
"Kit was his brother," he said at last. "Younger."
"Was."
"That's what they say in Savannah."
"And what do you say?"
Silas raised his eyes. There was a new caution in them, the caution of someone crossing a line and knowing it.
"I say there was a boating accident ten years ago. That the boat was found destroyed. That the family buried a closed casket." He paused. "And that Jago never believed it."
Rowena felt her own chest drop.
"Why did he never believe it?"
Silas shook his head slowly.
"Rowena. Not today. Not this. This isn't mine to tell."
"Silas."
"I've already said more than I should." He looked at the door. "Go home. Ask him."
"He won't answer me."
"Then come back here when he doesn't."
She got up. At the door, she stopped. She didn't look back when she said:
"Thank you."
Silas didn't answer. He kept looking at her coffee that hadn't been drunk, and for the first time in ten years he felt he might be betraying the right friend.
Back at the house near the river, Rowena found Neve in the kitchen, arranging sprigs of basil in a glass jar. The old housekeeper had her hair pinned up in a loose bun and wore the same gray apron as always. Rowena stopped in the doorway for a second before going in. Neve belonged to that house more than Jago belonged to it. If anyone knew, it was her.
"Neve."
The woman looked up. She smiled with that small smile she kept for Rowena.
"Good morning, dear. Shall I make some tea?"
"Neve, who is Kit?"
The smile disappeared so fast that Rowena almost doubted it had existed. Neve's hands gripped the jar too hard. One of the sprigs slipped and fell into the sink.
"Oh, dear."
"Please."
Neve looked at the door leading to the hallway. She looked at the window that faced the garden, where the morning jasmine was still closed. She looked at the counter as if the answer might be printed on the marble.
"It's not my place."
"I know it isn't." Rowena took a step closer. "But I've been sleeping in this house for six months, and I can't pretend anymore that I don't see. He avoids the terrace. You avoid answering. Mrs. Eudora has never said that name near me. And last night I asked my husband"—the word slipped out before she could choose another—"and he went silent."
Neve closed her eyes.
"He avoids the terrace," her voice came out broken, almost inaudible, "because that's where the boat set out that night."
Rowena didn't move.
"What night, Neve?"
"The night." Neve wiped her hands on her apron, a mechanical gesture. "The only night. The one this family could never bury properly." She turned her face aside. "Don't ask me for the rest, dear. Please."
Rowena swallowed hard.
"Mr. Kit," Neve continued, and now she was speaking to the basil, not to her, "had a way of laughing loud. Mrs. Eudora used to say he'd wake the whole house when he was a child." A small smile crossed her face and vanished. "It was a beautiful laugh. Too full for that mouth."
You have his laugh.
Rowena had to grip the counter. The air went out of her slowly. Her hand pressed on the cold marble. The certainty assembling inside her ribs like an old building rising from the dark—whose laugh it was, where the sentence came from, why Eudora had looked at her that way as she left that night at the ball.
"Thank you, Neve."
"Dear." The housekeeper held her arm for a second. Her fingers were firm and dry. "He suffered. Don't let anger make you forget that."
"I won't forget."
The weekend passed in a silence of war. Jago didn't try to enter the guest room. Rowena didn't come down for meals. Phelan carried trays in silence, left them outside the door, collected them untouched. On Saturday afternoon, Rowena wrote in her planner the list of everything she needed to trace. Payments. Names. Cities. Dates.
She started with the public library in downtown Savannah, where the old newspapers were kept on microfilm. She spent hours hunched over a reader that hummed softly, the smell of dry paper and old air conditioning filling the small room. She found the headline she knew existed. Quint Heir Missing After Coastal Storm. The photo was small, grainy. A young man smiling with his mouth open, his teeth a little crooked, his hair tousled by the wind. She looked at the face for a long time. There was something of Jago in it, in the shape of the eyes, in the line of the jaw. There was also that frank laugh Neve had described—too wide for a newspaper portrait, impossible to contain.
On Sunday, she cross-referenced information. She asked Phelan for old statements with some excuse, and the assistant, who already liked her more than he should, gave in without asking. She found regular payments going out of a parallel account of Jago's, monthly, always the same rounded amount, always to the office of a lawyer with no recognizable family name. She found receipts from a pharmacy in a small town she'd never heard of. She found, among the clippings Neve kept in an old envelope in the kitchen cabinet, a photo where a teenage Kit appeared beside an older man—the same face she'd recognized in the more recent records.
Marlowe called at three in the morning on Monday. Rowena answered on the second ring.
"Row, I had the strange feeling that you needed to laugh."
"Marlowe."
"Seriously. I was asleep, woke up out of nowhere, looked at the ceiling, and thought: 'this woman needs to laugh right now.'"
Rowena laughed. She laughed for real, mouth open, in the middle of the silent guest room. Then she started to cry and kept laughing, and Marlowe on the other end stayed quiet, waiting for the hurricane to pass.
"I found out the name," Rowena said, when she was able.
"The Kit guy?"
"He was his brother."
"Was."
"That's what Silas said."
Marlowe breathed in deep.
"And do you believe it?"
"I believe nobody pays for a pharmacy for a dead man."
Silence on the other end. Then:
"Row." Marlowe's voice was serious now. "Are you sure you want to go down this hole?"
"I already have."
"Then hold on tight."
Rowena looked at the planner open on the mattress. An address had appeared twice in the receipts. A small town a few hours by train from Savannah. A name that matched the man from the old photo. She circled everything in red pen and underlined the address three times, the pressure of the pen leaving a groove on the paper below.
On the other side of the house, Jago was sitting in the study with a glass of whiskey he hadn't touched in hours. He'd heard the taxi arrive in the morning. He'd heard the taxi leave. He'd heard Rowena ask Phelan for the statements with an excuse he knew because he himself was the one who'd used that excuse decades ago. He knew she was piecing it together. He knew she was going to find it. And, for the first time in ten years, he couldn't tell whether he was afraid of what she'd discover or afraid of what he'd feel when she knew.
He ran his hand over his forehead. His hand was trembling.
In the guest room, Rowena closed the planner. A name. An address. A small town. Everything that had come before—the old story, the charade at the ball, the coffee without a recorder—had been training for this decision.
She turned off the light. And, in the dark, she heard clearly the sound the empty chair made inside her now. It was no longer a creak. It was a whole name.
Chapter 4 — The Town With No Name
Silas gave in on Tuesday morning, and he gave in because Rowena stopped asking.
She walked into his office without knocking. She placed the open planner on his desk, the address circled in red, the name of the man from the old photo written beside it. She said nothing. Silas read it, leaned back against the chair, and closed his eyes for a moment, like someone who knew this moment would come sooner or later and had chosen too late.
"You got to this on your own."
"I get to almost everything on my own, Silas."
He opened his eyes. There was guilt in them now, an old guilt he no longer tried to disguise beneath the efficiency of the faithful assistant.
"If I complete the picture, Jago will kill me."
"Jago is already dying inside, Silas." She sat. "What do you want? To bury both of them?"
Silas kept looking at her for a length of time that seemed to stretch beyond how long silence usually lasts. Then he opened a desk drawer and took out a small card, no letterhead, with a name written by hand on one side and an address on the other. He slid it across the desk with two fingers, like someone handing something over and at the same time apologizing for having taken so long.
"The man who carries the money is this one." His voice came out low, almost hoarse. "He lives between Savannah and the town. He makes the trip by train once a month. Always on the first Saturday." He tapped the card lightly. "Kit lives in that town under another name. I'm not going to tell you which. That, he'll have to tell you himself."
Rowena took the card. She felt the paper weigh more than it should, more than any paper card should weigh.
"Thank you."
"Rowena." Silas leaned forward, his elbows on the desk. "If he doesn't want to come back, don't force it. He has that right."
"I won't force it."
"And, please." His voice nearly broke before reaching the end of the sentence. "Take care of Jago when you come back."
She tucked the card into the inside pocket of her bag. She stood up. She was almost at the door when he spoke again.
"Rowena."
"Yes."
"Why now? Why did you tell me now?"
He took a while to answer. When he answered, he looked at the window, not at her—at the street below, where Savannah went on ahead knowing nothing.
"Because I watched Jago spend ten years dying slowly, and I watched you arrive and him start to live again. And I can't watch him lose you thinking he's protecting you." He turned his gaze back to her. "Someone needs to break this cycle. You're the only one with the courage to break it."
Rowena nodded once. She left.
Outside, Savannah's air had that humid mid-afternoon heat, dense and green, with the smell of wet earth mixed with the distant salt of the estuary. She walked to the station without hurry, feeling the uneven paving under her shoes, the sounds of the city around her—the streetcar passing by, a child shouting in some backyard, the clinking of glasses in a corner café. She bought a one-way ticket for the four o'clock train and called Marlowe from the pay phone.
"I'm going."
"Alone?"
"Alone."
Marlowe paused for a long time, the kind that contains questions one decides not to ask.
"Do you want me to call someone if you don't come back in twenty-four hours?"
"Call Silas."
"Done."
Before boarding, Rowena stopped at the house near the river. She came in through the side door, went up to the guest room, threw some clothes into a small suitcase with the objectivity of someone who knows that thinking too much is a luxury that doesn't fit in the luggage. She went down to the kitchen, where the afternoon light came in slanted through the shutters and drew thin stripes across the stone floor, and wrote a short letter on a piece of writing paper. The letter had a single line.
I'll come back when I bring what he couldn't bring on his own.
She left the letter on top of the day's newspaper. She left.
Phelan saw her come down the stairs with the suitcase and asked nothing. She asked him to call a taxi. He called one. Before the taxi door closed, the assistant said, quietly, almost to himself:
"Have a good trip, miss."
She didn't correct him.
The train left Savannah at four o'clock sharp. Rowena chose a seat by the window and rested her forehead against the cold glass, and for a long time thought about nothing. She passed low houses, flooded fields, a smaller town with a rusty water tower in the center, a river that wasn't the Savannah, a line of cypresses cutting into the copper-colored sky of the late afternoon. The sun began to set slowly, tinting the landscape an orange that looked like a painting. She opened the planner and reread the address three times, as if she could forget it, and she knew she couldn't.
On the other side, at the house near the river, Jago arrived at seven at night and felt that something was missing before he looked in any direction. It was as if the house had changed weight—not temperature, not sound, weight. He passed through the living room, the kitchen, saw the letter on top of the newspaper. He read it. He read it again. And something in him collapsed with the same silent precision of a structure that gives way from within with no visible warning. It was the same old night returning to his nostrils. The smell of the sea after the storm. The taste of metal in his mouth. The physical, animal certainty that he had lost again—and that this time the punishment was proportional to the size of the mistake.
He let the letter drop. He sat on the kitchen floor. He truly sat, his back against the door of the low cabinet and his hands open on his knees, as if he didn't know what to do with them. He stayed there too long for it to be dignity.
Silas showed up half an hour later. He hadn't been called. He simply knew. He came in through the side door, found Jago on the floor, knelt beside him without hesitation.
"I told her."
Jago raised his face slowly. There was something in his eyes that wasn't anger—it was an exhaustion so deep it passed beneath the anger and reached a place the anger no longer reached.
"You told her what?"
"The name. The address. The man who carries the money."
Jago closed his eyes. It took him a full second to breathe again.
"I'm going to hit you, Silas."
"Hit me."
Jago didn't hit him. He clenched his fist, raised it, and the fist stopped in the air before touching anything. He lowered his hand slowly, rested it on his knee, and stared at his own fingers as if he didn't recognize them—as if that man sitting on the floor of his kitchen belonged to someone else.
"She went alone."
"She did."
"And if he doesn't take her in?"
"He'll take her in. He can't say no to anyone, Jago. He never could."
Jago swallowed twice.
"And if he takes her in and doesn't want to come?"
Silas didn't answer. There was no answer to that.
Jago squeezed his eyes shut. Inside, something in him was reconstructing the timeline with a precision that hurt—the train's schedule, the distance, the time she'd need to arrive, the night she'd spend alone in a town he knew from a map and from receipts, but never with his own eyes. He'd never gone. He'd never allowed himself to go. And now she was going for him, with the courage he'd traded for control, and he was sitting on the kitchen floor because he no longer knew what to do with his own body.
"Silas."
"Yes."
"If she comes back, I'll fix it."
"She'll come back."
"If she comes back."
Silas rested his hand on his shoulder and left it there.
"She'll come back, Jago."
In the small town, the train arrived at nine-twenty at night. Rowena got off with the small suitcase, felt the asphalt different under her feet—quieter, without Savannah's constant noise—and looked for a small hotel a block from the station, with painted wooden windows and a ceiling fan that turned slowly. She paid for one night, went up, didn't have dinner. She sat on the edge of the bed until dawn, with Silas's card in her hand and the address memorized.
In the morning, she followed the map on foot. The town was smaller than she'd expected—a main street with five shops, a central square with a bandstand painted white and peeling at the edges, low houses scattered along dirt roads where grass grew down the middle. The address led her to a modest, light-blue house, with a neglected front garden and a rocking chair sitting motionless on the porch.
She knocked on the door. Once, with her knuckle, firm.
A man opened it.
She recognized him before she looked properly. She recognized him by the shape of the eyes, by the line of the jaw, by the way he stood in the doorway—the same way Jago had of filling a threshold, that presence that filled the space even in silence. Except this man was thinner, harder, with a small scar crossing his left eyebrow and lighter hair, sun-bleached. He had the slightly crooked teeth she remembered from the newspaper photo.
"Kit."
He was paralyzed for a full second. Then he stepped back half a step.
"Who are you."
"Rowena Pike." She didn't move forward. "I've been with your brother for a while."
The silence that came after was dense and loaded. Kit rested one hand on the doorframe, as if he needed the support, his fingers gripping the painted wood.
"You shouldn't be here."
"I know."
"He sent you."
"He doesn't even know I came."
Kit looked at her for a long time. The physical shock was still in him, and the old anger rose automatically, and the fear hidden beneath the anger too, and there was something else, deeper, that he could no longer name well. Then he stepped aside.
"Come in."
The living room was small and clean, with that silence of a place where a person has lived alone for too long. There was a dark wooden table, two chairs, a sofa with worn fabric on the backrest, a shelf with few books and no ornaments. Kit pointed to one of the chairs. Rowena sat. He stayed standing, leaning against the wall, arms crossed.
"Talk."
She talked. She talked for a long time, without embellishing, without crying. She talked about the investigators Jago had hired over the years. About the money he left with lawyers who had no names. About the medications paid for by third parties, the debts settled before they became dangerous. About the empty chair no one explained and no one removed from the living room. She talked about Eudora, about the way she never said his name, about the way once, on the porch, she had murmured about a lost laugh. She talked about the terrace Jago avoided. She talked about the envelope he'd hidden, about the control he confused with love, about the old fear he carried because he thought that if he let go, he'd lose everything again.
Kit listened without interrupting. When she finished, he stayed silent for a while. Then he laughed—a short, dry laugh, almost a grunt.
"He spent ten years looking for me."
"He did."
"Without stopping."
"Without stopping."
"And he never came himself."
Rowena hesitated.
"He thought you didn't want to be found by him."
Kit closed his eyes. Inside, something that had been hard for ten years began to crack, slow and inevitable as a crack in stone. He swallowed once, swallowed again.
"He wasn't wrong."
"He was." Rowena's voice came out lower. "He just didn't know he was."
Kit peeled himself off the wall. He went to the window, looked at the neglected garden for a long time, at the grass growing between the paving stones. Then he turned around.
"I'll make coffee."
They spent the afternoon and the night talking. On the porch, with cups that went cold twice and the sound of crickets filling the dark around them, Kit spoke aloud for the first time about what he felt. He said he'd fled because he thought he wasn't important to anyone. That on that night he'd asked his brother for help and his brother hadn't come, and he understood, in that instant, that his world was worth less than a meeting. That when the boat began to crack in the storm, he had two seconds to choose between swimming or sinking, and he chose to swim because the anger was greater than the exhaustion. That when he came out the other side, on a stretch of beach he didn't even know where it was, he made the decision to disappear because he thought no one would miss him enough to really look.
"And he looked," Kit said, looking at the bandstand in the square in the distance. "All these years."
"All these years."
Kit ran his hand over his eyes, slowly.
"He made a mistake out of fear, Kit. Not out of choice." Rowena spoke low, looking at the same point he was. "He's afraid of you all the time. Of what happened, and of what he didn't do."
Kit laughed again, this time without the dryness of before, almost with affection—an affection he clearly hadn't expected to feel.
"You talk about him as if you knew him."
"I know him."
"You love him."
She didn't answer. She didn't need to.
They stayed silent for a while, with the warm wind passing through the branches of the old magnolia growing in the corner of the garden. Then Kit spoke, looking at his own hands.
"If I come back, I come back once. No promises. If it doesn't work, I leave again, and this time no one finds me."
"Agreed."
"And I don't know if I'll be able to look at him."
"You don't need to know that now."
Kit nodded slowly, like someone making a deal with himself as much as with her.
In the morning, he took a small suitcase. Very small. Rowena looked at that suitcase and understood the size of what he carried—not in what was inside, but in how little there was. Before they left for the station, she sent a single message to Silas from the telegraph on the corner.
We arrive tomorrow. Prepare Eudora.
While they waited for the train on the wooden bench of the town's only platform, Kit looked at the horizon and said, without turning his face:
"Rowena."
"Yes."
"If you brought me here for him." He swallowed. "Thank you."
She didn't answer. She simply rested her hand over his hand, and his hand was cold, and hers was too.
The train whistled in the distance.
Chapter 5 — Ten Years in a Single Room
Jago didn't sleep.
The house near the Savannah River was so silent that he could hear his own pulse in his throat. Sitting on the study floor, leaning against the shelf, his suit rumpled and his tie loose since the small hours, he counted time by the way the light came through the window—first blue, then gray, then a timid gold that arrived too insulting for the weight of the day.
Rowena's letter was still folded in his lap. He no longer needed to reread it to know what was written. "I'll come back when I bring what he couldn't bring on his own." Each word weighed more than the last. Each word opened a door he'd kept closed for ten years and pushed him inside it with no choice, no warning, without even letting him breathe first.
Inside, Jago had the cold certainty that the punishment had finally arrived. Not in the form of scandal, not in the form of death, not in the form of a headline. In the form of a woman with an ink scar in the Sunday paper going after what he hadn't had the courage to bring. And, in the most cowardly part of him, a low voice said that this scar could open again. That this time the woman, too, might not come back.
Silas came in at seven without knocking. He brought coffee Jago hadn't asked for and a silence Jago didn't want. He stood in the doorframe, waiting with that lawyer's patience of someone who'd already seen too many clients on the floor of his own office.
"Talk," Jago said, without looking up.
"She arrived. She's with him."
"How do you know?"
"She sent me a message. Short."
Silas hesitated, and at the bottom of that hesitation there was an old guilt the lawyer had decided to carry in silence until now. He held out the phone. Jago read the line on the screen and took a full second to breathe again.
We arrive tomorrow. Prepare Eudora.
He closed his eyes. He felt the room spin as if someone had flipped the floor. There was a man inside him who wanted to yell at Silas for giving up the thread; there was another, deeper, who wanted to kneel and give thanks. The first won for half a second. Jago stood up, grabbed Silas by the lapel, and stopped his fist in the air before it closed. His hand trembled. It never trembled.
"Don't hit," Silas said, very low. "You're not a man who hits."
"I'm not a man of anything right now," Jago answered, and let go of the lapel like someone releasing a rope too hot to hold.
They stood still. Outside, a bird insisted on a whistle of three repeated notes, without tiring, without mercy. Jago looked at his own reflection in the glass of the picture frame and recognized neither the disheveled hair nor the civilized despair his eyes held.
"Prepare her," he asked, and his voice didn't quite obey. "Prepare my mother."
"I will."
"Silas." Jago swallowed. "Thank you."
Silas looked at him, long. He said nothing. He left.
It was Phelan who arrived first at the Quint mansion. Eudora was having tea in the small parlor, the newspaper still untouched on the white linen napkin. The matriarch looked up and read, in the young assistant's posture, that something was coming before the words. She didn't ask. She waited, with that old skill of letting silence do the work.
"Mrs. Rowena asked that you come to the main hall at five," Phelan said, with the trained delicacy of someone delivering news he didn't understand. "And Mr. Jago as well."
Eudora folded the newspaper carefully, as if it might break. Her hand, when it rested on the table, had a strange lightness for the weight of the day.
"That's all?" she wanted to know.
"That's all."
The matriarch didn't smile. She didn't protest. She nodded once and turned her gaze to the window, where the garden trees swayed in a breeze no one inside could feel.
The train arrived at four. Rowena got off on the Savannah platform with the same small bag she'd left with and a thin man beside her who insisted on keeping three steps of distance, like someone still deciding whether to stay or turn around and disappear again.
Kit had his head down, his cap pulled low, his backpack held tight against his chest like a shield. Every sudden noise in the station made his jaw clench. He stopped twice on the way to the taxi, and both times Rowena pretended not to see, continuing to walk at a pace that demanded nothing.
"We can go back," she said, at the third stop, without pressure. "If you want to go back now, we go back. I'll take you back and won't tell anyone you set foot in Savannah."
Kit swallowed.
"It's not that."
"It's what, then?"
"It's that I forgot what the smell is like."
Rowena looked at him. There was an eighteen-year-old boy inside that thin man, and the boy was blinking hard not to cry on the sidewalk of a station full of strangers. She didn't touch his arm. She just stood there, at the same height, waiting.
"The smell is the same," she answered. "Magnolia. And old money."
Kit laughed, a quick laugh, almost a sob.
"Ah. So it's the same."
They got into the taxi.
Rowena told the driver to stop at the mansion, not at the house near the river. Kit raised his face when the façade appeared, that heavy iron gate he knew better than any other place in the world. He stared at it for a long time. A leaf came loose from one of the tall trees and fell on the hood of the car with a lightness that seemed calculated. No one said anything.
"I don't know if I can," Kit said, low.
"You can make it to the door. After that, we decide."
"And if they hate me?"
"They don't hate you. They don't know how to hate you."
Kit didn't answer. He opened the car door and got out slowly, like someone stepping onto a floor that might not hold his weight.
The main hall smelled of magnolia and old money. The afternoon came in through the tall windows in bands of golden light that crossed the marble and stopped at the empty chair near the window, as if they knew exactly where to land.
Rowena went in first. She deliberately chose that place—not the living room, not the library, not Jago's study where everything had begun to fall apart. The hall. The same room where one night, months earlier, a woman in a borrowed dress had held the face of a silent man and kissed him in front of three hundred people. The smell was the same. The light came in at the same angle. The chair was still empty.
Rowena positioned herself exactly where she had stood on that other night, as if she were marking a fixed point on a map only she knew how to read. Kit stayed one step behind, still in his cap, still with his hand closed around the strap of the backpack.
Phelan opened the double doors.
Eudora came in first. The matriarch noticed Rowena, noticed the figure behind her, and for a moment—tiny, almost invisible—her spine seemed to give way a millimeter. She restrained it. She kept her chin high. She crossed the hall to the center and stopped, waiting.
Jago came in after.
And his world stopped turning.
There was a thin man in the hall. Thin, with his cap pulled low, a small scar on his eyebrow, his shoulders narrower than memory had drawn them. A man who slowly lowered the cap, revealing his short hair, and looked up—and it was the gaze of the eighteen-year-old boy who had taken a boat out alone on a stormy night ten years before.
Jago said nothing for a length of time no one there could measure.
He took a step. Then another. Then he stopped. Inside, it was as if a dam had given way—the old guilt, the anguish of the last two days, the fear of losing Rowena, all mixing into a single hot point in the middle of his chest, too hot to be borne with composure. He felt his throat tighten. He felt his hands go cold. He felt, with brutal clarity, that the punishment hadn't come. That life, against any deserving, had given something back.
"Kit," he said. And his voice came out choked, ridiculous, the voice of a big boy that didn't fit that man. "Kit."
Kit dropped the backpack on the floor. The thud was dry on the marble.
"I ran away," he said, and his mouth trembled. "I ran away because I thought I wasn't important to anyone."
Jago closed his eyes. A tear fell before he could command his body to hold it back. Then another. He didn't wipe them.
"I spent ten years," he answered, "trying to fix the worst choice of my life."
Kit shook his head, incredulous, and the shaking became a tremor, and the tremor became a short, dry sob. Jago crossed the rest of the hall in three strides and hugged his brother any which way—a crooked hug, without elegance, without the composure all of Savannah knew. A hug that had waited ten years to happen and that now happened all at once, clumsy and urgent. Kit was slow to return it. When he did, he gripped Jago's coat as if he were holding on so as not to fall.
Eudora, at the back, had stopped breathing.
The feared matriarch, the woman who demanded proof of love in the middle of a crowded hall, the woman Rowena had met with eyes of steel, took a step forward. Then another. And, with no audience, without elegance, without any of the posture all of Savannah knew by heart, Eudora Quint knelt on the marble floor beside her lost son. The tweed dress folded like an old cloth. Her hands sought Kit's face without the courage to touch it right away.
"My boy," she said, and the knife-edged voice became a voice with no edge. "My boy."
Kit turned to his mother and couldn't speak. He just rested his forehead on her shoulder. Eudora closed her arms around her son with a care no one, ever, had seen that woman show.
Rowena watched from the doorway.
Inside, she felt her body weigh from two sides at once. There was relief, yes—the real, physical relief of seeing the family finally occupy the space it needed to occupy. And there was an exhaustion she hadn't noticed accumulate until the adrenaline dropped for good. The last forty-eight hours had taken everything. And there was, too, something hard in the middle of her chest, something that made her understand that this moment belonged to those three, and that she didn't need to sign it.
She took a step back. Then another. She closed the double doors in silence.
The veranda facing the Savannah River had a warm late-afternoon wind. Rowena leaned against the cold stone parapet and watched the water darken little by little, the reflection of the light going away in wide, slow bands. She didn't cry. She felt inside her chest a new stillness, the kind that only arrives after days that are too long, when the body finally drops its guard because it has nowhere left to put the tension.
Jago found her there ten minutes later. He'd washed his face. His suit was still rumpled. His eyes, still red, settled on her before any words and stayed there, heavy, as if they needed to confirm that she was real and that she was standing.
He stopped three steps away. He didn't come closer.
Any word, he realized, would be too small. Any sentence would sound like a clause, and he'd already spent six months signing clauses with her without asking permission for it.
So he stayed quiet. He just stood there, looking at her as if it were the first time.
"You brought him," he said, at last.
"I brought him."
"I don't know how to thank you."
"Don't thank me. It's not the kind of thing you thank someone for."
He nodded, a nod that was almost a bow. The river below kept darkening, indifferent to the weight of that veranda. A gull crossed the sky in a lazy arc. Rowena felt the warmth of his body near hers without needing to look to be sure. There was magnetism there, the same as always—the same that had risen up her back on a gala night with three hundred people watching. And, beside him, there was a new distance, a distance she'd decided to preserve for now.
"I need to sleep alone tonight," she said.
Jago took a second. He didn't protest. He didn't make a face. He didn't embed any request disguised as agreement.
"That's fine."
Rowena turned her face to him.
"It's the first time in six months you've given me an answer without packaging."
He almost smiled. Almost.
"I'm learning," he answered, very low.
There was exhaustion in his face, but there was also something open she'd never seen there before. He wasn't the man of ice. He wasn't the man of calculated control. He was a man who had seen his brother alive after ten years and no longer knew what to do with his own chest.
"Tomorrow we'll talk," she said.
"Tomorrow I'll ask," he said. "If you'll let me."
She nodded.
He raised his hand toward her face, stopped halfway, and lowered his arm. The interrupted gesture weighed more than the touch would have weighed. Rowena, inside, understood that he had understood.
She went down the veranda stairs without hurry. Phelan took her to the house near the river. Jago went back inside, to the family he had forgotten how to embrace without a contract.
That night, in the kitchen of the house near the river, Rowena drank a glass of water in silence and looked at the phone on the table. There were no messages. For the first time in six months, no one knew where she was—because no one had been put in place to know. She turned off the kitchen light, and the darkness arrived with a calm that seemed, at last, deserved.
On the other side of the city, in a room he hadn't been in for ten years, Jago Quint personally made up his brother's bed—folded the blanket, opened the window two fingers to let the air in—and sat on the hallway floor until dawn, with the door ajar, listening to Kit sleep.
Chapter 6 — The Chair With an Owner
Sunday morning arrived at the house near the Savannah River with a low, wary light that came through the cracks in the curtains like someone asking permission. Rowena was in the kitchen, barefoot, with a cold mug in her hands. She hadn't slept well. The too-wide bed had felt wide in a new way, without anger and without peace, only with a question waiting for its answer outside the door.
She heard the engine before she heard the footsteps. A car stopped on the gravel. The door closed with the care of someone who knew the whole house was listening.
Jago appeared in the kitchen doorway with the cream envelope open in his hand. He had no jacket. He had the rumpled shirt of someone who'd spent the night sitting on a hallway floor. He stopped two steps from her, as if asking permission to cross that last stretch of floor.
