I woke before her, like always. The thin July light slipped through the half-open curtain and fell in a slanted stripe across Iolanda's bare shoulder. I stayed still, propped on my elbow, watching her face as she slept. Her mouth slightly parted, her dark hair spread across the pillow on my side of the bed, one hand curled near her chin like a child's. The air conditioner hummed low, almost imperceptible, and there was a scent of jasmine coming from the window—she'd insisted on planting jasmine on the balcony, and I'd let it happen without arguing, which said a lot about what she'd done to me. There was a time when I couldn't sleep in that suite. Now it was the opposite—I couldn't leave it without first making sure she was still there. I admitted, inwardly, something I still felt embarrassed to say out loud. I was happy. The word sounded strange in my head, almost indecent for a man like me. Happy. I got up slowly, pulled the sheet over her shoulder so the AC wouldn't hit her skin directly, and went down barefoot. The cold marble of the staircase under my feet was the same as always, but even that seemed different on the mornings she was upstairs. The mansion's kitchen at that hour was mine alone. Donna Chiara was still in the back garden cutting rosemary—I could hear, from far off, the sound of her scissors against the thick stems. The dogs slept piled up near the service door. My father's old clock kept the slow rhythm of six-twenty. I grabbed the coffee tin, measured it into the moka pot the way Iolanda had taught me without realizing she was teaching. Two sugar cubes. A drop of milk. Never two. Bastian came in through the service door with his planner under his arm and stopped talking before he even started. He looked at me, looked at the cup in my hand, looked back at me. "Good morning," he said, dry. "Good morning." He set the planner on the counter with the ceremony of a man delivering an official document. A second of silence. Two ticks of the clock. "Should I bill the household for the Don becoming a barista?" I didn't answer. The corner of my mouth betrayed half an intention of a smile and I corrected it before he noticed. He noticed. Pretended not to. That's how we'd worked for ten years. "What's on the schedule?" "Fausto asked for half an hour this afternoon. The elders sent word about the dock. And Donna Ottavia—" he paused, chose the word "—requested a family meeting at noon. Formal sitting room." I looked at him. "Did she request a meeting or announce one?" "Announced." I filed the information in the place where I store the things I haven't yet decided what to do with. My mother had come back from Naples two weeks earlier than planned, without warning and without explanation, and I'd expected her to arrive with her temper sharpened, with a list of grievances, with that silence of hers that cuts deeper than any shout. I hadn't expected her to request the formal room. The formal room was for weddings, funerals, and contract executions. My mother knew very well what each setting said before a single word was spoken. "Tell her we'll be there. Iolanda and I." Bastian nodded, brief. "Anything else?" I raised the cup. "Take this up before it gets cold. Set it beside the bed. Don't say anything. She hates being woken." He took the cup with both hands, like someone carrying a museum piece, and left. I stayed a second in the empty kitchen, listening to the distant sound of the turned-off moka and the tick-tock of the clock filling the silence he'd left behind. I leaned my back against the marble counter and looked up at the high ceiling. There was a scene that came back on its own, without permission, some mornings. I'd seen Iolanda for the first time in the photos Fausto brought me that same afternoon, months ago. The flash from the neighborhood paper's photographer had caught her face at the exact moment the bell tolled for the last time, and none of the guests knew how to look at her properly. I did. I saw in the photo the exact moment her face understood that Adelmo wasn't coming, and I saw that she decided, right there, that she wouldn't cry in front of those people. That day I decided two things, and I'm still keeping both. To get her out of the trap the Serravalles had set for her. And to never tell her that I'd seen her cry in a white dress. I heard light footsteps on the stairs—the fourth step always creaked, and the sound reached me with the punctuality of an agreed-upon signal. I came back to the present. I left the kitchen, crossed the high-ceilinged corridor, and passed through the hall to the garden. She'd be by the camellias—she was always by the camellias before eight, said it was the hour when the flowers breathed. She was. In a long nightgown, hair pinned at the nape, watering can in her hands. The morning still had that damp July light that makes everything seem slightly unreal, and she moved among the plants with the ease of someone born there, though I knew that three months ago she barely knew the camellias' name. I came up behind her without a sound and kissed her neck right where I'd learned she shivers slightly. She shivered. "You shouldn't do that when I'm holding water." "If you soak the camellias and me along with them, I won't be offended." She laughed, softly, and turned her face until she tucked her chin against my shoulder. Her hair smelled of rosemary, probably because of Donna Chiara, who insisted on leaving dried sprigs in every room of the house as if that were protection enough against anything. "Your mother is up to something." "My mother is always up to something." "No. Today she's up to something." I looked at her profile against the morning light. She'd learned to read the house the way one reads the Bible—word by word, skipping nothing. If she said my mother was up to something, my mother was up to something. "Formal sitting room, noon," I said. "We go together." "Together." It was just one word, and it landed there like a signature at the bottom. I took the watering can from her hand, set it on the ground, and pulled her to me by the waist. Beneath the cotton of the nightgown there was a small secret only the two of us knew, and I could count on my fingers the people outside that house who also knew. Iolanda. Me. The doctor. Donna Chiara suspected, but Donna Chiara suspects everything and is rarely wrong. "Do we tell them today?" she asked, against my chest. "My mother?" "Your mother." I thought a second. "After the meeting. We'll read the room." She agreed without speaking. I felt her hair against my mouth and decided that this was the last morning I'd put anything off. After lunch I'd tell Fausto to pull the old contract out of the drawer and destroy it with me, in front of me, at my signature. There was a clause in it that I'd written with my own hand in another life, and I'd carried the postponement of that clause the way a man carries a small stone in his shoe—not annoying enough to make him stop, but always hurting. Today I was taking out the stone. "Come," I said. "Eat something before you face the matriarch." She laughed again. She laughed more since she'd been mine. I'd learned to listen to her laugh the way one learns a language he never cared to learn and ended up in love with its sound. We went inside, and the garden door closed behind us with the soft sound of old wood settling into its frame. Four hours until noon. A whole lifetime until noon.

The formal sitting room was the only room in the mansion where I'd never felt whole. Ceiling too high, mahogany shelves with books no one read, two sofas of dried-blood-colored velvet, and a rug so pale it felt cruel to step on. The air in there had that specific smell of a shut-up room you only open on occasion—mothballs mixed with old varnish and something floral trying to mask the other two. Ottavia liked that room because the room made an impression. That's why she chose it. She sat with her back to the window, and the noon light came in behind her and turned her into a dark silhouette against the gold. A staging choice, the choice of someone who knows how to stage. Fausto stood behind her, leaning against the shelves, his head lowered in a way I'd never seen in the consigliere. Don Vittorio, Don Emiliano, and Don Sisto stood, the three of them with that old bearing of men who'd learned to stand for hours at wakes for friends they themselves had ordered killed. I came in hand in hand with Ludovico. I felt his finger squeeze mine for half a second, almost involuntary, and let go. He read the room before I did. "Mother." "Son." Ottavia moved her head a centimeter in my direction. "Iolanda." I sat on the sofa across from them, him at my side. I crossed my hands over my knee. There was a dark leather folder on the little table between us, and the folder was closed, and the folder had the crest of the lion with the broken crown blind-embossed on the cover. My stomach sank half an inch. I recognized that crest. It was the same as the letter that had arrived months ago, on a night when I still didn't understand much of what I was living, the one Ludovico had put in the drawer without explanation. Ottavia opened the folder with the slowness of someone who knows no one will interrupt. She took out a sheet. Just one. "Before my son marries you twice," she said, without looking at me yet, "I thought it better you know the whole contract." She pushed the sheet with two fingers to my edge of the table. I didn't move. Ludovico moved—half a second too late. I felt his hand hesitate on my knee and settle there. I looked at the sheet. It was paper yellowed at the edges. Family letterhead. My signature at the bottom, that shaky signature I'd learned to make at the registry office months ago with my head spinning from hunger and humiliation. And above my signature, in the third clause at the bottom, there was a paragraph written by hand. By hand. Not typed. By hand, in the writing I knew from notes in the corners of library books, from messages left at my bedroom door in the first month, from the dedication he'd written in a Pavese novel and pretended hadn't been him. I read it once. On the day the heir is born, the marriage is dissolved. The wife leaves with a fortune corresponding to half the Don's net worth. Alone. Without the child. Full custody remains with the Caracciolo family. I read it again. The words didn't change. I raised my eyes to his profile. He was looking at the sheet. Not at me. At the sheet. And his mouth opened, and his mouth closed. He didn't say no. Ottavia smiled without smiling. "Keep your word, son. It's written in your own hand." I heard my own name sound inside my head as if called from far away. Iolanda. Iolanda, listen. That writing is his. That paragraph was him. That paragraph had been there the whole time, beneath everything, while I learned his coffee, while he pulled the sheet over my shoulder, while we stood in the garden with our hands inside each other's as if that were real. He finally turned to me. His eyes were the exact color of the morning we walked into the camellia garden, and in that color there was now a terror I'd never seen on his face, not in the small hours of the library, not on the night of the attack in the street. "Iolanda." "No." I stood. My legs answered. It was the morning's only luck. I smoothed my skirt with both hands as if that mattered, and I looked at the three elders, one by one, and then at Ottavia, and lastly, very lightly, at Ludovico. "You two fit together," I said. "The both of you." My voice didn't shake. To this day I don't know how. I walked out of the room, though everything in me wanted to run. My heels struck the marble of the hall as if they belonged to another woman, and the other woman climbed the stairs far too slowly, one step, two, three, and on the thirteenth I had to put my hand on the railing to keep from fainting. The wood was cold under my palm. I focused on that. Only on that. In the bedroom corridor I didn't go to the suite I'd been sharing with him for three months. I went to the door at the end, the door I hadn't opened since we'd decided to sleep together. The white paint around the knob was slightly faded—I'd noticed that detail on the first day and completely forgotten it afterward. The old contract room. I turned the knob. The air inside was still, warm, with the smell of old wood and the dust that gathers in places no one enters anymore. The bed made as on the day I'd left, the lamp on the nightstand with its bulb off, the straw chair in the corner where I'd sat on the night of the storm thinking that was the worst that would ever happen to me. I closed the door behind me and the click of the latch sounded loud through the whole house. I sat on the edge of the bed. The mattress was cold. I laid my hand flat over the belly no one yet knew existed. And for the first time in three months, I said quietly, to no one and to him: "We're going to be okay." It was the first lie I told my child.

The afternoon fell over the old contract room without me moving from the edge of the bed. I watched the sunlight cross the floor in a long rectangle, shrink, stretch, shrink, vanish. The room turned blue. Then gray. Then black. I didn't turn on the light. There was a sound I'd learned to ignore over the past months—the sound of the house itself breathing, pipes, wood expanding, Donna Chiara's distant footsteps in the pantry. I listened to all of it with a new attention, like someone arriving at a place for the first time. The smell of cold wax on the floor. The creak of a window at the end of the corridor. The whole house sounding like a living organism that didn't know I was falling apart inside it. Maybe it was the first time. I started doing something I didn't want to do and did anyway. I started rereading the last few months backward, each gesture of his, and fitting them into the shape of that handwritten paragraph. The coffee. Two cubes, a drop of milk. He'd noted it on the first day, had Bastian note it, kept the order alive in the mansion's kitchen and at the Café Vecchia Roma. I'd thought it was the small tenderness of a man who doesn't know how to say the big things. And what if it was just care of an investment? A man tends his cattle with the same constancy. With the same cold, calculated attention, no affection, only foresight. The coat over my shoulders on the rainy night. I'd kept that coat at the back of the closet, in the first week, the way one keeps a relic. The dark, heavy fabric, with his scent mixed with the wet cold of the street. And what if it had been the calculation of a man who knows an elegant gesture buys cheap loyalty? The small hours of the library. Him broken in the armchair, the untouched glass, the talk about his dead brother. And what if every sentence of that had been rehearsed to soften a woman who still thought she was merchandise, and the handwritten paragraph proved she was? I listened to that voice until I almost believed it. Then I listened to another. The newer voice, the one three months old, the one saying I wasn't an idiot. I'm not an idiot. I'd seen his face in that room and I saw terror, not a game. I saw a man who didn't know what to do with his own hands while his mother slid the paper across the table. I stayed between the two voices until my mouth was too dry to talk to myself. He knocked on the door for the first time near nine at night. Three short knocks, his way, without pleading. "Iolanda." I didn't answer. "I'm not coming in. I just want to know if you've eaten." I didn't answer. He stayed there. I heard his breathing on the other side of the wood, held back, controlled, the way he controlled everything—except the things that truly mattered. I heard him let out the air like a man giving up on a sentence before saying it. I heard the footsteps drawing away far too slowly, as if he wanted to give me time to change my mind. I slept dressed on top of the comforter. I dreamed of the church in Providence. In the dream, the bell wouldn't stop tolling. I woke with the sun on my face and my body heavy. I got up, went to the adjoining bathroom, washed my face with cold water until my cheeks stung. In the mirror I looked at the woman with swollen eyes and didn't recognize her at first glance. Second glance, I did. It was still me. Just more tired. He knocked twice on Friday. In the morning, in the voice of a man who'd decided not to impose. In the afternoon, in a hoarser voice, as if the hours had scraped something loose inside. Both times, he said the same thing. "I'm here." Both times I didn't open. Donna Chiara came up with a tray near noon. She didn't knock. She left the tray on the floor, against the door, with a note under the plate. I waited for her footsteps to fade and opened the door the smallest crack. I pulled the tray inside. Soup. Bread. A pear. The note had three words in her round, motherly hand: Eat for the little one. She knew. Of course she knew. Donna Chiara knew everything in that house. I ate the whole bowl of soup without tasting it. I ate the bread. I ate the pear down to the core. It was the first thing I did for the little one, and the second thing I did for myself. Nerina called near five. I answered because it was her. "Are you alive?" "I am." "Are you eating?" "Now I am." "You want me to come over?" I thought a second. The idea of having someone in the room felt too big to bear, like a strong light in an eye used to the dark. "Not today." "Tomorrow." "Tomorrow I'll say." She was silent long enough for me to know she was thinking about not hanging up. And then she said her thing. "If you need wine and a butter knife, you know where I live." I laughed. It was half laugh, half sob, but it was. It was the first time in twenty-four hours that my face made a movement that wasn't pain. "I know," I answered. I hung up. Lay down again. And this time I managed to cry without being angry at myself for crying.

I spent all of Friday outside that door. Not the whole time. There was a chair in the corridor, a moss-colored velvet armchair no one had used in years, and I sat in it when my legs got tired of standing. I came back to knock when I thought she might hear. I never left the floor. Bastian came up twice with office questions and went down with short answers. Fausto asked to speak with me over the intercom. I sent word that not today. I leaned my forehead against the door's wood near the end of the afternoon and spoke low, without calling her name. The wood was cold. I let it stay that way. "I wrote that before you existed in my life." I stopped. "Before I knew you'd exist in my life." I stopped again. "That doesn't fix anything. I know." On the other side, her silence had weight. I could tell her silences apart. There was her thoughtful silence, her wounded silence, her sleeping silence. That was a new silence. A silence that was weighing on me in return. I didn't ask to come in. I didn't have the right. The clause I'd written with my own hand, in the week I decided to buy her from the Serravalles, was the only piece of my history with her that she didn't know, and that's why it was the only piece in which she could still believe I'd planned to lose her from the start. If I forced that door now, I wouldn't be opening a door. I'd be confirming the paragraph. I went back to the study at eight. I called Bastian. "You know this house." "I do." "Find every copy of that contract. My mother's, Fausto's, the one I keep in the safe, the one the registry sent to the secretariat and never came back. All of them. Bring them to me." He stood still half a second, enough to register that I wasn't asking, and left without a single question. I opened the drawer of my father's study and took out something that had sat there for three months. The handwritten draft of the clause. Yellowed paper, black ink, my writing. I set it on the desk and looked at it the way one looks at a dead animal he hadn't the courage to bury when he should have. There was a promise I'd made to my own conscience three months ago, on the morning Iolanda had agreed to sleep at my side without a contract. I promised myself I'd erase that clause one day. I'd put it off. I'd thought I'd have time. I'd put it off like a man putting off a doctor with chest pain. And now the pain had come for her, not for me, and that was what truly hurt in the whole story. I put the draft under the blotter. It wouldn't burn alone. It would burn in front of the person who needed to see it. I went up again. I leaned my forehead against the door one last time that night. "Goodnight, Iolanda." On the other side, a second. Two. And a whisper so low I could have invented it. "Goodnight." I didn't invent it. She said it.

Saturday was born gray, with a pale light striking sideways at the shutters and streaking the floor with opaque bands. I got up before the heat pressed in, took a long bath, chose a light dress, pinned my hair up with the two hands that no longer trembled so much. I opened the door of the old contract room and stepped out for the first time in almost forty hours. I didn't go looking for him. I knew that at nine he'd be with Fausto in the downstairs office. I went down the opposite side of the staircase and headed to the winter garden, where Donna Chiara's bromeliads caught the light through the glass and everything seemed quieter than the rest of the house. Donna Chiara appeared with a tray before I asked. Chamomile tea, a small piece of bread with butter, an orange peeled into segments. She set the tray on the wicker table, stood a second, dried her hands on her apron. She didn't look at me straight on. "He loves you, dear," she said, looking at the bromeliads. "Men like him are stupid about the things they love." I didn't answer. She didn't wait for an answer either. She went back inside. I drank the tea slowly, feeling the warmth of the cup in my palms. I ate the whole piece of bread. I ate the orange. And as I ate, I understood something that had taken forty hours to reach me. If he wouldn't tell it, I'd go after it. I'd done this before. Months ago I'd gone to find the truth about Adelmo with my own hands, and the truth gave me back everything. Maybe the truth about that handwritten paragraph was also stored somewhere in the city, in an archive, in someone's head, in his writing on some other paper. Fausto, probably, knew. The Café Vecchia Roma, where everything had begun, held the beginning. And if there was a beginning I still hadn't fully understood, it was mine. I took the phone from my pocket and messaged Bastian. Car tomorrow morning. Early. Just the two of us. He answered in less than a minute. Yes. He didn't ask where to. He didn't warn the boss. Bastian was Bastian. I spent the rest of the morning in the winter garden. Ludovico didn't come looking for me. I knew he knew I was there, on the other side of the glass. I knew he was waiting for a signal from me, and I knew he wouldn't force one. There was something new in that silence that I'd learn to read in the coming days, and that something was respect. He was letting me choose the distance. At the end of the afternoon I went up again. I passed the door of his study. The door was ajar, a thread of yellow light leaking through the gap. He was sitting at the desk, no tie, his shirt rolled to the elbows, a pile of folders on the right and a pile of folders on the left, as if the work had come back to fill every hour I was away. He was reading a sheet with an attention that hurt to look at. He raised his eyes and saw me. He didn't move. Didn't smile. Just looked, the way he looked when he was holding something too big to fit into a sentence. "Goodnight," I said. "Goodnight." That was all. I continued down the corridor. I went to the suite that was ours and didn't go in. I went to the old contract room. I lay dressed on the comforter, hand on my belly, staring at the white ceiling until it stopped spinning. I closed my eyes. If there was anyone who'd learned to hunt her own truth in the past months, it was me. Tomorrow I'd begin.

Sunday began cold, the way Providence knows how to be cold even in summer. The light came through the window of the old contract room in a hard way, without generosity. I'd slept little. My hand stayed open over my belly, the way it had already become habit these past two days, and I wondered, in the second before waking fully, whether my child already knew that his mother had made a plan with no one for a hunt. I got up slowly. Put on dark pants, a turtleneck, flat shoes. Nothing that suggested Donna. Nothing that suggested bride. Just a woman with an address in her head and a question that would no longer fit inside her. Bastian waited in the car, outside. He didn't ask a question. He just opened the rear door and kept looking straight ahead while I got in. The radio was low, an old Italian song I knew without remembering from where. "Café Vecchia Roma," I said, buckling the belt. "Then the Ranieri office. Then an address I'll give you later." He nodded half a millimeter with his head. Started the car. He spent the afternoon on the phone with Fausto, arranging the following night. We went back to the mansion at the start of Saturday evening. I had the handwritten draft in the pocket of my dress. I gave it back to him before going into the main room. Neither of us said anything about it. It was agreed without words. The main room was full. Don Vittorio, Don Emiliano, and Don Sisto seated in the high chairs at the back. Ottavia in a side armchair, spine straight, hands crossed over her lap, with the same polite contempt of the first day I set foot in that house. Fausto standing near the hearth. Bastian leaning against the wall by the door. I came in without taking Ludovico's coat off my shoulders. He'd covered me again, in the car, without saying anything, and I hadn't given it back. I pulled my part of the contract from my pocket, the copy Donna Chiara had brought back to me in the old room, on the first day of mourning. I tore it in two in front of them. I didn't shout. I let the pieces fall on the rug. "I'm not leaving with any fortune," I said. "Nor with any child under my arm. I'm staying. Not for the door. For the house." Ottavia opened her mouth. Closed it. "The clause was mine," he said. "The power it gave me over her was mine. Now it belongs to no one." He held the sheet out toward the hearth. The paper caught fire at the edge with an almost impatient haste, as if it too had waited too long. He held it until the last safe instant, then let go. The ashes fell over the stones of the hearth. Bastian stood near the window of the breakfast room with the newspaper folded under his arm and his usual face, which meant something had happened. I learned that early—when his face had no expression at all, it was because he was holding one. Two days had passed since the night of the elders. The mansion breathed a different air, one I was still learning to recognize. Donna Chiara served coffee with her chin higher. The corridors, which had always seemed too empty, now had people who greeted me by the name the family had reluctantly accepted, and there was a small but real difference in the way that greeting arrived—less ceremony, more person. Ludovico raised his eyes from the newspaper. "Providence?" he asked. "Boston," Bastian answered. "South dock. Ranulfo Serravalle. Found this morning with problems." "Problems of what order?" "The order that doesn't ask for a second visit, sir." Ludovico turned his eyes back to the newspaper as if Bastian had commented on the weather. He turned the page with a calm I no longer found cold—I found it deliberate. There was a difference. I picked up my cup with both hands, felt the warmth enter my fingers, and asked nothing more. Someone in Providence would no longer send word with dead flowers. That was all I needed to know. The rest I left with him. Nerina appeared in the doorway carrying a white box wrapped in green ribbon. "Cake," she announced, in that way she had of turning every entrance into an act. "Before anyone asks why." Bastian got up and pulled a chair for her, without a word, without taking his eyes off the window. Nerina looked at the chair. Looked at him. There were stories that weren't mine yet. Seven weeks later, the garden was covered in flowers. I didn't wear a veil. I chose a simple dress, cut at the waist, no train and no adornment I'd need to carry. Nerina had helped me tie the white ribbon around the stems the night before, crying while she laughed at herself, which was exactly her way of being happy for someone. I walked to the altar alone. There was no one to give me away, and even if there had been, I wouldn't have accepted. No one gives away a woman who has already given herself. The morning sun came in slanted between the rows of chairs, and the shadows of the rose stems on the arch fell in long streaks over the stone path. I heard my own footsteps. They were the only ones. Ludovico waited beneath the arch with his father's old watch on his wrist and his arms at his sides, not crossed, hiding nothing behind them. He was in a dark suit, no tie. When he saw me arrive, he swallowed. I saw the movement in his throat. I also saw the effort he made not to let more than that show, and I saw that the effort wasn't enough. Bastian was in a tie as few times as I'd ever seen him—a dark gray tie, meticulously knotted, the only sign that that morning was different from all the others. I didn't look at her once, and when the priest began to speak, I realized I also wouldn't need to anymore. The priest said short things. Ludovico said his vow so low that the camellias barely heard it. But I heard it. He said he'd learned, too late, that love isn't guaranteed with a clause. That he'd spend the rest of his life without writing any exit door for me. That if one day I wanted to leave, it would have to be me walking, because he'd no longer build the exit with his own hands. I said my vow looking at him. "I chose to stay. I'll keep choosing every day." He kissed me with no contract between us. No door. With the morning sun on my back and the smell of camellia in the air, and his hands on both sides of my face as if I were the only stable thing in a world he'd spent his whole life trying to control. Winter came heavy in Providence. Enrico was born on a late-January dawn, with snow falling outside the window in thick, quiet pieces, no wind, as if time had decided to be gentle at that hour. Labor was long. Ludovico didn't leave the room once. He held my hand through every contraction, his forehead pressed to mine, murmuring things in Italian that I'd begun to understand because of how much he murmured near me in hours without witnesses. Outside, Nerina waited with Donna Chiara and an absurd basket with enough sandwiches to feed a whole battalion, because that was her way of enduring the wait. Bastian stood in the corridor with the gun hidden under his jacket, protecting something that had no enemy that dawn. Enrico came into the world with a strong cry, the size the family had waited for over three generations. The obstetrician placed him on my chest first. He stopped crying almost at once, as if he recognized the place. I stayed looking at him, that tiny piece of a person with wet black lashes and hands closed into small fists, and I found no word sufficient for what I felt. I didn't try. Ludovico took him afterward. He held his son in his arms with both hands open, the small body lost in his large arms, and stayed motionless for a few seconds just looking. And that was when he wept. He didn't hide it. He didn't turn his face. He didn't run his hand over his eyes as if wiping away something else. He wept looking at his son, his chin trembling and the tears running down the line of his jaw without him making any gesture to stop them, with a stillness that was only his and that I'd never seen in any of the faces he used in the world. "He has your nose," he said low, to me, when he found his voice. "Poor thing," I answered. He laughed in the middle of the weeping. He leaned his forehead against mine, that gesture that had become our private language. Then he leaned his forehead against Enrico's too, closing the triangle among the three of us, and we stayed like that for a time I couldn't measure. "Enrico," he said. "You were right." I'd suggested the name weeks before, on an ordinary night while we waited for the snow to stop outside. Enrico, in honor of his brother, the one who'd died at nineteen in a story he told me only once, slowly, without adornment. Ludovico had looked at me for a long time when I said it, without saying yes or no, as if he needed to carry the name a little before giving his answer. Only now, with his son in his arms and the weeping he didn't hide, was he saying yes. Six months after the birth, the morning in the suite was one of the calmest the mansion had ever seen. Enrico slept in the crib in the room next door, and the monitor beside my side of the bed purred with his breathing, that sound that had become the soundtrack of my small hours. The June light came through the cracks in the shutters in wide, warm bands. Ludovico came in carrying a tray with two cups, barefoot, no jacket, in the previous day's shirt.
